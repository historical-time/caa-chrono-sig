  2. Enrico Crema | Introduces a Bayesian alternative to traditional aoristic analysis for handling chronological uncertainty in archaeological data. Implemented in the R package baorista.
  3. Caitlin Buck, Miguel Juárez | Introduces Bayesian analysis; first published in Spanish (2020), edited by Juan Antonio Barceló and Berta Morell.
  4. Vasiliki Andreaki, Juan Antonio Barceló | Proposes a framework to study how archaeological sites form and change over time, using 3D geometry and depositional events to reconstruct their chronological sequence.
  5. Stephen Stead | Describes the key concepts in the CRM family of standards for documenting temporal data.
  6. Komlan Midodzi Noukpoape, Philippe Lanos, Philippe Dufresne | Revises the “event date model” of Lanos and Philippe (2017) and proposes improvements to it.
  7. Diamantis Panagiotopoulos, Martina Trognitz | Reviews the state of the art in Minoan chronology and proposes guidelines, best practices, and formalisms to improve it.
  8. Isaac McIvor et al. | Applies the ChronoLog software—used for Chronological Network Analysis—to the study of Māori genealogies (whakapapa), combining oral histories and radiocarbon data.
  9. David Falk | Presents and applies the Groundhog software—another Chronological Network Analysis tool—for improved reconstruction of Ancient Near Eastern chronologies.
  10. Eythan Levy | Proposes a new typology of temporal relations tailored for archaeological applications.

S35: Chronological Modelling III: a Round Table on Time in Computational Archaeology

Round table, 2h30 and 4 tracks:

  1. Epistemology of archaeological time
  2. Archaeological time in practice: cross dating, anchor dates, cultural periods
  3. Formats, standards and interoperability
  4. Mathematics, Algorithms and Software

This round table aims to discuss the current challenges and future perspectives in modeling time in archaeology. Each of the following topics will be discussed for 30 minutes and chaired by a different scholar (Joan Antonio Barceló, Keith May and/or James Taylor, Florian Thierry, Joe Roe).

  1. Epistemology of archaeological time | What is the nature of archaeological time (in comparison to historical time)?
  2. Archaeological time in practice: cross dating, anchor dates, cultural periods | How do archaeologists effectively align multi-sourced chronological data (stratigraphic layers, artifact seriations, etc.)?
  3. Formats, standards and interoperability | On reference systems, thesauri, data fusion, etc.
  4. Mathematics, Algorithms and Software | On existing IT-based methods for computing temporal data and emerging challenges.

A final 5–10 minute session will be dedicated to discussing the possibility (or not) of developing a Temporal Information System (similar in concept to a GIS for geographical data).
